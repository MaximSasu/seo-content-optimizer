# Keyword Research

Goal: find the searches that real potential customers type, that you can realistically rank for, and that are worth ranking for. Then map them to pages.

## The process

### 1. Seed the topic

Start from what the business sells and the problems it solves. Write 5 to 15 seed phrases in the customer's language, not the company's jargon. A roofer's customer searches "roof leak repair cost", not "asphaltic membrane remediation".

Sources for seeds:

- The site's own products, services, and category names.
- Competitor sites: their nav, blog titles, and page headings.
- Customer language: reviews, support tickets, sales calls, Reddit and forum threads, the "People also ask" and "Related searches" boxes on Google.
- If you have web access, search a seed term and harvest the autocomplete suggestions and the "People also ask" questions. These are real queries.

### 2. Expand into a full list

For each seed, branch out:

- **Modifiers**: best, cheap, near me, for beginners, vs, alternative, how to, what is, review, 2026.
- **Long-tail variants**: longer, more specific phrases. They have lower volume but higher intent and are far easier to rank for. "wireless earbuds" is brutal; "best wireless earbuds for small ears running" is winnable and converts.
- **Questions**: who, what, when, where, why, how. These map directly to blog content and to featured snippets / AI Overviews.

### 3. Classify by intent

Tag every keyword with its dominant intent, because intent decides what kind of page you build:

| Intent | Searcher wants | Page type to build |
|--------|----------------|--------------------|
| Informational | To learn something | Blog post, guide, FAQ |
| Commercial | To compare before buying | Comparison, "best of", review |
| Transactional | To buy or act now | Product, service, landing page |
| Navigational | A specific brand or page | Home, brand, login page |

If you are unsure of intent, look at what currently ranks. Google has already decided: if page one is all blog posts, it is informational; if it is all product pages, it is transactional. Build the type that is already winning.

### 4. Judge each keyword on three axes

- **Relevance**: will the searcher become a customer or a genuine audience member? A high-volume keyword that attracts the wrong people is worthless.
- **Volume**: roughly how many searches per month. Use a keywords tool if one is connected (Ahrefs, Semrush, Google Keyword Planner). If you have no tool, estimate relative volume from autocomplete prominence and reason transparently that the numbers are directional.
- **Difficulty**: how hard the top results are to beat. Proxy signals when you lack a tool: are the top results big established brands or smaller sites? Is the content thin or comprehensive? How many referring domains do they likely have? Newer or smaller sites should chase lower-difficulty long-tail terms first and earn authority before attacking head terms.

The sweet spot, especially for a younger site: high relevance, decent volume, low difficulty. Long-tail and question keywords usually sit here.

### 5. Cluster and map to pages

Group keywords that share the same intent and would be answered by the same page. One cluster equals one page, targeting one primary keyword plus its secondary variants.

This prevents cannibalization (two pages fighting for the same term) and builds topical authority (clusters of related pages signal expertise on a subject).

Then sketch the internal structure: a pillar page for the broad head term, supporting posts for each long-tail subtopic, all linking to the pillar and to each other where relevant.

## Output format

Deliver a sortable keyword map table:

| Keyword | Intent | Est. volume | Difficulty | Why target it | Page type | Cluster |
|---------|--------|-------------|------------|---------------|-----------|---------|

End with a short recommendation: which 3 to 5 keywords to attack first and why (usually the best relevance-to-difficulty ratio), and the suggested pillar-and-cluster structure.

## If a keyword tool MCP is connected

Check for Ahrefs, Semrush, or similar connectors. If present, pull real volume, difficulty, and SERP data rather than estimating, and say you are using live data. If not, estimate from SERP inspection and autocomplete, and be explicit that the figures are directional rather than measured.
